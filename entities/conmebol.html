01 AT A GLANCE

Small membership, outsized pull

CONMEBOL governs South American football across ten member associations — the oldest confederation in the world (1916) and by far the smallest by membership, yet one whose heritage clubs and national teams command global broadcast value far above their numbers. It owns the Copa América at national-team level and the Copa Libertadores and Copa Sudamericana at club level.

The commercial trajectory is steeply up. Total income reached a record ~$869m in 2024, and club prize pools have hit successive highs, with the Libertadores champion prize set at $24m. A new 2027–2030 club-rights cycle was awarded in late 2025 to Globo and ESPN/Warner Bros. Discovery after the confederation's most competitive tender to date.

03 OPERATING REALITY

How the confederation actually functions

CONMEBOL's revenue roughly doubled between 2023 and 2024 — extraordinary, but partly the Copa América cycle inflating a single year and partly the structural escalation of club-competition rights. Locking the 2027–30 cycle early de-risks the medium term, though the headline value of that deal was not disclosed.

Its ten-nation base is tiny, but concentration of heritage in Brazil and Argentina gives the confederation global broadcast pull far above its membership weight. The 2030 centenary is more symbolic than commercial: it secured three ceremonial matches, not the tournament, after losing the full-hosting bid — ambition that outruns the actual hosting allocation.

04 STRATEGIC POSTURE

Direction in 2026

The posture is aggressively expansionist — record prize money, an early-locked rights cycle, and a centenary narrative positioning South America at the centre of the 2030 story. The open commercial question is how much of the 2024 record is structurally repeatable in non-Copa-América years, and whether prize-money escalation can continue without straining the smaller federations' competitive relevance.
