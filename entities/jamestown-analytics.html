01 AT A GLANCE

The vendor that thinks like an owner

Jamestown Analytics sits in the football-tech layer but does not look like the rest of it. There is no shrink-wrapped product, no per-seat licence, no industry conference booth. The firm operates from a small office in Camden, north London, with a headcount kept deliberately low. Each engagement is a long-form, exclusive partnership: a client club retains Jamestown on a multi-year basis; the algorithms are tailored to that club's tactical, financial, and league-specific constraints; and the same model is then refused to direct competitors. Hearts of Midlothian, for instance, hold the exclusive Jamestown partnership in Scotland.

The firm's reach is narrow by design. Public partnerships span Brighton & Hove Albion (the founder's own club), Royale Union Saint-Gilloise (Belgium), Como (Italy), Ipswich Town (England), Hearts (Scotland), and Castellón (Spain). Each is an underdog or upwardly mobile club in its market. Each is overperforming the table position its wage bill would predict. Each piece of public commentary by the firm or its principal frames the work in the same terms: small, calibrated, and concentrated where the information advantage is largest.

03 OPERATING REALITY

What the firm actually does, and how

The operating model has three integrated functions. The first is squad appraisal: at the start of an engagement Jamestown ranks every player on the client's roster against positional comparators across the global database, producing a calibrated baseline of where the squad sits today. The second is tailored recruitment search: the client supplies criteria — tactical role, age band, budget, contract horizon, league preference — and Jamestown produces ranked shortlists derived from a model trained against that client's specific profile. The third is head-coach and management search: the same data plumbing applied to coaches' tactical fingerprints and historical performance signatures. Hearts' two-step appointment of Derek McInnes is the most-publicised example.

The firm's commercial structure is its under-appreciated competitive moat. Jamestown does not sell to direct league competitors. The exclusivity is enforced contractually rather than informally: Hearts holds the exclusive Scottish partnership; Union SG holds it in Belgium; Castellón in the Spanish lower divisions. The economic effect is a deliberate scarcity model — the firm could plausibly take on five or ten times the current client base on capacity grounds, but does not, because the value proposition collapses if the same algorithm is informing both sides of a transfer market.

The financial economics are not public, but the structural picture is straightforward. Annual retainers in the low-to-mid eight figures sterling, a small Camden headcount with most cost in senior data and football-domain talent, no marketing spend, no platform infrastructure of the kind StatsBomb or Hudl carry. Reported margins are high. The firm's principal beneficiary is Tony Bloom personally; the model is not capital-constrained.

04 THE BLOOM ORIGIN

From Starlizard to the football-tech layer

The firm's origin runs through Tony Bloom's professional gambling career and Starlizard, the Mayfair-based syndicate Bloom built around a proprietary statistical model of football match outcomes. Starlizard's edge — and the source of the personal wealth Bloom subsequently used to acquire Brighton in 2009 — sat in the same data infrastructure that, repurposed, became Jamestown. The same event-data feeds, the same modelling discipline, the same iterative refinement against outcome data — turned from match prediction to player and coach evaluation.

The Starlizard heritage is the part of the story that explains why Jamestown is structurally different from a sport-tech startup. The firm did not need to raise capital, did not need to build distribution, did not need to engineer a SaaS product. It started from a position of fully-funded data infrastructure and a principal who already understood, from the betting side, how small a calibrated information edge has to be before it compounds.

Bloom's most consequential 2024 move — the £9.86 million convertible-loan investment in Hearts of Midlothian — formalised what had been an arms-length consulting relationship into an owner-aligned one. The investment was paired with the exclusive Jamestown partnership in Scotland and an explicit framing, in trade-press interviews, of the Hearts engagement as a sister-club model in the Brighton / Union SG mould. That framing matters: Jamestown's most engaged clients are now also its founder's owner-aligned clubs, with all the alignment-and-conflict implications that comes with it.

05 STRATEGIC POSTURE

Direction in 2026

The stated direction in 2026 is selective European expansion under the existing exclusivity model. Public commentary by Bloom and by client-club executives suggests an active pipeline in the lower divisions of Spain and Italy, in the secondary Scandinavian leagues, and possibly in MLS. The firm is unlikely to expand into the major-five top flights aggressively because the exclusivity rule binds — Brighton holds the Premier League slot, Como holds Serie A, no further engagement is possible in those leagues without disturbing existing clients.

The model-architecture posture is unresolved. Jamestown's IP is tailored bespoke modelling; the broader sport-tech direction is toward foundation-model substrates that the largest in-house teams are building. The competitive question for 2027–2028 is whether bespoke calibration continues to outperform generalist large-model approaches in the specific use-cases Jamestown serves — tier-two recruitment, tactical-fit coach search, opposition analysis at sub-Premier-League budget — or whether the in-house substrate path overtakes the vendor model. The honest read is that nobody, including Jamestown, knows.
