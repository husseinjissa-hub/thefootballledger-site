At a glance

From MLS saviour to trophy-venue owner

AEG is Philip Anschutz's privately held global venue and live-entertainment company, built around owned trophy assets such as London's O2 and Los Angeles' Crypto.com Arena, plus the AEG Presents promotion business. In football it was central to MLS's survival, at one point operating up to six clubs — but it has since sold all but one, retaining only the LA Galaxy.

Its identity has inverted. Where AEG once ran a venue-management roll-up, that business — ASM Global — was sold to Legends, completing in 2024, so AEG no longer owns the 300-plus-venue network that older profiles attribute to it. The unspoken strategic variable is succession: Anschutz is 86, and any future sale narrative is more credibly an estate event than an operating decision.

Operating reality

How the business actually functions

AEG's identity has shifted from a venue-management roll-up to a concentrated owner of a handful of irreplaceable trophy assets — the O2, Crypto.com Arena — plus the AEG Presents promotion business; the management-at-scale game now belongs to Legends via ASM. Many dated profiles wrongly attribute ASM's 300-venue scale to AEG, a correction worth carrying.

The football story is largely legacy: AEG kept MLS alive in its lean years, then harvested most of that portfolio, retaining the Galaxy as a marquee asset rather than a growth platform. No audited public revenue figure exists, and third-party trackers conflict by more than twofold, so any revenue number should be treated as an estimate.

Strategic posture

Direction in 2026

The posture is a going concern with no confirmed active sale, divesting non-core and concentrating on owned trophy venues and AEG Presents. The open questions are whether any current sale or partial-stake exploration exists (none is confirmed), AEG's true consolidated revenue, the succession plan for the Anschutz-owned assets, and its direct 2026 World Cup exposure beyond LA-region venues.
