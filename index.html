The Stack at a Glance

The stack. One pyramid.

Money flows down. Influence flows up. Disruption now flows sideways. Layer 3 — clubs and multi-club groups — sits at the operating centre of the system, but the layers above and below define its constraints and opportunities.

Layer 01

Governing
bodies

The pyramid. FIFA, the six confederations, and national associations sit here. They set the rules of competition, license referees and agents, run the marquee national-team and continental-club tournaments, and distribute the rights revenues attached to those competitions.

The moves that

The map shows where capital and influence sit today. The Ledger reads where they are moving — where the next operating playbooks will be written, where the white space sits, and where the strategic questions for the next decade are emerging.

Section A · Macro

The shifts
that cut across
the ecosystem.

These are the structural shifts shaping the industry's next decade. Each is well known in isolation. The argument here is that they are connected — and that the most consequential operating decisions of the next five years sit at the intersections.

01
Capital · Governance · Operating Models

From trophy assets to operating businesses

For 15 years, sovereign and family capital bought football clubs as identity assets — political prestige, brand projection, soft power. Sport produced returns the way Picassos do: unmodelled and emotional. That era is closing. PIF's April 2026 strategy reset removes sport from its priority ecosystems. Apollo's €2.9bn control deal at Atlético is structured like a leveraged buyout. The next decade rewards operators, not collectors.

Contrarian read
The consensus says "sport is now investable." The sharper read is that the binding constraint is the supply of leaders who can hold financial discipline and football culture at once — not capital, not governance frameworks. Capital is abundant; dual-fluency leadership is scarce.
The strategic question
"No institutional owner has yet produced both sporting competitiveness and a defensible return at a big-five club. Does Apollo–Atlético become the first proof case — or confirm that football pays one way or the other, never both?"
Signals
PIF strategy reset · Apollo–Atlético · Kingdom Holding–Al Hilal · FSG appointing CFO-track operators
02
Ownership · Strategy · MCO

Multi-club ownership has won.
The integration question is open.

Multi-club ownership has become the default architecture for ambitious football capital. Nearly half of Big Five clubs sit inside groups, and the deal pipeline shows no sign of softening. The question that defines the next decade is no longer whether the model spreads — it is whether the groups that have spread can run their portfolios as platforms, and whether the regulator lets them try.

Contrarian read
The consensus says MCO is football's future. The sharper read is that value comes from running clubs as one platform, not from assembly — and most assemblers are not yet investing in the integration capability that would prove it.
The strategic question
"Does the regulator shift from policing MCO as an integrity risk to a framework — like Formula 1's audited operational separation — that lets integration create value for owners, operators, and fans?"
Signals
CFG integration ROI · Group-HQ headcount at RedBird, BlueCo, INEOS · BlueCo blind-trust outcome · Friedkin Article 5 exposure · UEFA Article 5 evolution · first audited group synergies
03
Geography · Cycles

USA — the next mega-cycle

The 2026 World Cup (USA / Mexico / Canada) is the inflection point. MLS valuations have doubled in five years; NWSL franchise prices have crossed $100m; USL Premier launches in 2028; the NWSL is now PE-investable. Apple-MLS is the world's only football DTC at scale and will be the case study for whether DTC economics can replace pay-TV. The 2027–2030 chapter is where the next round of senior US-soccer operating talent will be tested.

Contrarian read
Most assume the World Cup will create the boom. The sharper read is that most of the value is captured pre-tournament (already priced in by 2025–26) and the post-tournament period will sort winners from losers — a buyer's market for operators, not assets.
The strategic question
"Does the capital now entering US soccer build a third pole — competing with the European Big Five and the Gulf wave — or fund a still-secondary market that absorbs capital and supplies talent without competing at the top?"
Signals
Apple-MLS renewal economics · NWSL valuations · USL Premier launch · Bay FC and Boston Unity SC trajectories

04
MENA · Operating Era

MENA's second wave — operations, not signings

Phase 1 (2021–2025) was acquisition: marquee signings, club takeovers, the launch of LIV Golf and the Esports World Cup. Phase 2 (2026 onwards) is value-realisation, privatisation, governance, commercial sophistication, stadium delivery, and academy infrastructure. Eleven new stadiums for 2034. Privatisation of Saudi Pro League clubs underway. The 2027 AFC Asian Cup co-hosted with the UAE. The operating profile required for Phase 2 — financial discipline, club governance, infrastructure delivery — is materially different from the dealmaker profile that defined Phase 1.

Contrarian read
Most public commentary on MENA football still describes the Phase 1 narrative — oil money, marquee signings, large public bets. The sharper read is that Phase 2 is technocratic, and the operating profile required is fundamentally different: financial discipline, club governance, commercial sophistication, infrastructure delivery.
The strategic question
"What does the operating playbook for a privatised Saudi Pro League club look like, and which group writes it first?"
Signals
PIF strategy reset · Kingdom Holding–Al Hilal · SPL privatisation timeline · 2027 Asian Cup operating org · NEOM SC build-out
 In production
05
Attention · Formats · Gen-Z

Sportainment as a Gen-Z bridge

Kings League, Baller League, TST, Icon League, World Sevens — now mainstream-funded by Michael Jordan's Courtside Ventures, Verance Capital, and now adjacent to Gamma Waves Partners. 2025 Kings World Cup final drew 3.5m unique device interactions. These will not replace traditional football. They will reshape what fan engagement means at traditional clubs — shorter formats, creator-led storytelling, native-digital broadcast, blurred line between athlete and entertainer.

Contrarian read
The hype frames sportainment as competitive to traditional football. The sharper read is that traditional clubs that absorb sportainment lessons fastest will widen the gap against clubs that dismiss it.
The strategic question
"Which top-flight clubs are running the sportainment playbook on their own platforms, and which are leaving the format to outsiders?"
Signals
Kings League MENA edition · Baller League US growth · Gamma Waves' sports-IP thesis · iShowSpeed-Baller League · CJEU ruling residue

06
Women's Football · Investability

Women's football institutionalises

A standalone investable category for the first time. Avenue Sports Fund's NWSL entry, Apollo's continued NWSL exposure, Mercury13 (Chiellini-backed MCO), World Sevens Football, FSG's Boston Unity SC, Liverpool FC Women, CFG adding women's clubs systematically. NWSL franchise valuations crossed $100m. Women's football MENA is greenfield — almost no capital, regulator-supportive, low competition.

Contrarian read
Consensus says "growth category." The sharper read is that women's football MENA is the single largest white space in global football — and the first credible operator wins category-defining position.
The strategic question
"Why has no major MCO yet built a serious women's football platform in MENA, given the structural conditions that favour it?"
Signals
Mercury13 deals · NWSL valuations · Boston Unity SC · Saudi Women's Premier League · UEFA Women's CL economics
 In production
07
AI · Operations · Edge

AI-native football operations

Every major workflow is being rebuilt around foundation models — recruitment (Twelve Football's WhatsApp scout chatbot, SkillCorner's tracking AI, Comparisonator's universal performance currency), video analysis (computer-vision auto-tagging at Sevilla, Brighton's psychological profiling via Inside Out Analytics), injury risk (Zone7), fan content personalisation. The clubs that integrate first will have a 5–10% sustainable competitive edge for half a decade. This is exactly the kind of systemic change a strategy director leads.

Contrarian read
The dominant framing of AI in football is as a tooling question. The sharper read is that it is an operating-model question — clubs that use AI to redesign workflows beat clubs that bolt AI onto existing workflows.
The strategic question
"In which club workflows does AI change who makes the decision, rather than only how quickly the decision gets made?"
Signals
SkillCorner's 250-team footprint · Twelve Football's WhatsApp scout · Sevilla AI-led scouting · Brighton/Inside Out · Liverpool's "football IQ" model
08
Broadcast · Media · Rights

Football’s Netflix never arrived.
What did, hasn’t settled yet.

A decade of consensus held that streaming-native platforms — Apple, Amazon, Netflix, DAZN — would consolidate football's broadcasting rights into the industry's Netflix moment. Across Europe, North America, and MENA, the experiments have run long enough to judge. None produced that outcome. What replaced it is three configurations competing for the next cycle: tech platforms bundling football inside a broader proposition, established multi-distribution operators with broad sports portfolios, and partnership-shared rights structures.

Contrarian read
The consensus expected a single streaming-native buyer to win tier-1 rights. The sharper read is that streaming platforms participate only where football is content inside a broader proposition — never as a standalone destination product at tier-1. The unit economics that broke the single-buyer model are not temporary.
The strategic question
"Which two of the three configurations survive the compression — and is the next structure decided by M&A between the players rather than by who bids highest at auction?"
Signals
Premier League 2027 tender · Apple–MLS 2029 renewal · Streaming/pay-TV M&A · DAZN capital structure · Ligue 1+ first cycle
Layer 01 · Section B

shifting

2026 (US/MEX/CAN, 48 teams), 2030 (centenary, 6 hosts across 3 continents), 2034 (Saudi). The next decade is a continuous mega-event delivery cycle. FIFA's operating org has to triple in technical capability — and most of that hiring is in non-traditional FIFA disciplines (digital, hospitality, infra delivery, regulator liaison).

Trend 1.2

UEFA under legal pressure

CJEU ruling (Dec 2023) on Super League removed UEFA's prior-approval monopoly over new competitions. Recent CAS rulings on multi-club ownership cases consistently weaken UEFA's enforcement lever. UEFA's response — squad-cost rules, MCO tightening — is increasingly a rear-guard action.

Trend 1.3

National regulators arrive

UK's Football Governance Act (2025) created the Independent Football Regulator. Other European jurisdictions watching closely. For the first time in modern football, club ownership is subject to a domestic regulatory layer above league rules — fitness-and-propriety tests now have legal teeth.

Watch: IFR enforcement actions · French / German parallel proposals · MCO approval cases

Trend 1.4

AFC + SAFF professionalising fast

The 2027 Asian Cup (Saudi/UAE) and 2034 World Cup are forcing AFC and SAFF into operating models that look more like UEFA or FIFA than the traditional Asian-confederation governance template. SAFF in particular is hiring senior commercial, technical, and infrastructure leaders at a pace previously unseen in the federation. The result is a federation rebuild happening in real time alongside the tournaments it is preparing to deliver.

Layer 03 · Section B

Clubs & MCO —
integration wins

The acquisition phase of MCO is over. The integration phase has barely begun. Strategy and operating talent at MCO group HQ is the highest-leverage seat in football right now.

Case study · Layer 3

Barcelona's crisis and recovery

Between 2020 and 2022, FC Barcelona ran into the most severe balance-sheet crisis at any major European club this century. Messi left as collateral. The Laporta reset registered the next squad. Four years on, the recovery is partial, the debt is real, and the strategic question is open: did the club solve the problem, or just push it ten years into the future?

Watch: Camp Nou full-capacity reopening · Espai Barça debt-service · 2027–28 La Liga commercial cycle · academy pipeline retention
 In production
Trend 3.1

Group-HQ talent arms race

CFG, FSG, RedBird, BlueCo, and INEOS Sport are each building 30-to-80 person group-HQ teams covering football operations, commercial, data and technology, finance, and legal. Most of these teams did not exist three years ago. The recruiting pattern is unusual for football: the hires are coming primarily from outside the industry, from management consulting, technology, and adjacent sectors.

Trend 3.2

Mid-tier MCO blow-up risk

Eagle Football, 777 Partners (collapsed), and several smaller MCOs have shown the model can fail under cash-flow stress when too many leveraged acquisitions happen too fast. Expect 2–3 more public failures by 2028, creating distressed-asset opportunities for better-capitalised platforms.

Women's clubs added systematically

Most major MCO groups now treat women's clubs as a portfolio addition rather than a CSR add-on. CFG, FSG, Liverpool, Mercury13. The economics still don't fully justify it on a stand-alone basis — but the brand and pipeline arguments increasingly do.

workflows

The fastest-moving layer. Foundation models rebuild recruitment, video, medical, and fan workflows. The clubs that adopt first build durable competitive edge.

Trend 8.2

The data-led underdog arrives in Europe

Hearts at the top of the Scottish Premiership. Como back in Serie A's upper half. Union Saint-Gilloise champions of Belgium. Bodø/Glimt in the UCL Round of 16. A small set of mid-budget clubs is punching above its weight by buying data-led recruitment as a service. Jamestown Analytics is the through-line. The Brentford effect, generalised — and a live test of how durable the data edge remains once the rest of the league catches up.

Trend 8.1

The AI-native football-tech stack

Foundation models are rebuilding the four dominant football-tech workflows in parallel: LLM-native scouting interfaces (Twelve Football, Comparisonator, SkillCorner), computer-vision auto-tagging that compresses 4–6 hour video sessions into near-real-time output (StatsBomb, Hudl), injury-risk prediction on training-load and biometric data (Zone7, Kitman Labs, Orreco), and the contested edge of psychological profiling from match footage (Inside Out Analytics). One stack, four use cases, one durable competitive edge for the clubs that adopt early.
