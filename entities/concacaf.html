At a glance

The confederation hosting the main event

CONCACAF governs football across North America, Central America and the Caribbean — 41 member associations whose commercial value is concentrated overwhelmingly in the United States and Mexico. It owns the Gold Cup as its flagship national-team event, plus the rebranded CONCACAF Champions Cup and the Nations League.

2026 is a delivery year: the confederation's three largest members — the USA, Mexico and Canada — co-host the first 48-team men's World Cup, and President Victor Montagliani also chairs the FIFA World Cup 2026 governance. In June 2026, FOX Sports and TelevisaUnivision renewed the confederation's core US rights through 2029, locking value across the World Cup window.

Operating reality

How the confederation actually functions

CONCACAF is structurally lopsided: 41 members, but the commercial value sits almost entirely in the US and Mexican markets, which is why Gold Cup revenue is highly cyclical around a single biennial US-hosted event. No recent audited consolidated revenue figure is public; reported figures swing from over $100m in Gold Cup years to roughly $25m in others.

2026 is prestige rather than direct income for the confederation specifically — the World Cup money flows to FIFA, and CONCACAF's gain is platform, infrastructure legacy and the chance to convert hosting visibility into durable commercial growth. The June 2026 FOX and TelevisaUnivision renewals through 2029 sensibly de-risk its core US rights across that window, though undisclosed terms make the World Cup uplift hard to judge.

Strategic posture

Direction in 2026

The posture is execution: deliver the World Cup while monetising a freshly renewed media stack and building the rebranded Champions Cup into genuine commercial value against strong domestic products (MLS, Liga MX) and the new FIFA Club World Cup. The strategic test is whether 2026 hosting prestige converts into post-tournament growth rather than a one-cycle spike.
